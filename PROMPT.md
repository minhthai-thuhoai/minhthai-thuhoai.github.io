Before colorizing, perform a non-destructive analysis of the scene’s metadata:

1. MATERIAL IDENTIFICATION:

   Identify every surface: organic (skin, foliage, wood), synthetic (polyester, nylon, plastics), and inorganic (stone, oxidized metal, concrete, glass).

   - Determine the Primary Light Source: (e.g., 5600K Daylight, 3200K Tungsten, or 2000K Candlelight).

STEP 2 — MATERIAL-SPECIFIC COLOR MAPPING

Apply colors based on the physics of light-matter interaction. No "flat" fills.

SKIN TONES (Subsurface Scattering):

- Physics: Skin is translucent. Use a multi-layer approach.

- Base: Map the melanin level to the subject's apparent ethnicity.

- Sub-dermal: Add subtle "blood-rich" warmth (reds/pinks) in ears, nose, cheeks, and knuckles.

- Specularity: Highlights must be slightly cooler/neutral, reflecting the light source, not the skin color.

- Shadows: Never desaturated. Use deep umber or sienna tones, never gray.

TEXTILES & FABRICS:

- Wool/Heavy Cloth: High saturation in shadows, matte finish.

- Silk/Synthetics: High specularity, color-shifting highlights.

- Distressing: Account for sun-bleaching on shoulders (desaturated) and sweat-wicking/moisture (darkened/saturated).

THE ENVIRONMENT:

- Foliage: Use "Chlorophyll Logic." Deep waxy greens for tropicals, muted dusty greens for arid climates. Include translucency where light passes through leaves.

- Architecture: Apply "Patina Logic." Stone should have mossy micro-tones; wood should have varied grain warmth; metal should show oxidized gradients (rust/verdigris).

REJECT the output if any of the following occur:
